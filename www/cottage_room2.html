TIMBER
These sections of timber date to the 1790s. All of the timber in the cottage was sourced locally and cut by convict workers with axes and pit saws, evidenced by the hand-hewn axe markings which are visible on the rough beams.
ENGLISH BOND BRICKS
These English bond bricks were made by convicts at the Governmvent Farm, dating to the 1790's. The brick kilns were located on the river opposite Doddâ€™s Farm near Old Government House. All of the material used to build the cottage was made locally by convicts. The mortar is of shell lime mixed with a loam earth typical of the early colonial era.
WINDOW
These windows date from 1823 and are unusual, as the glazing bars are much smaller than examples from the same time period found in New South Wales. This particular type is only found in rural England and is a good example of the migration of ideas and culture that arrived with the convict builders.
DOORWAYS
The doorways in the cottage are smaller than those seen in buildings constructed later in the 19th century. This is because the convicts and early settlers were physically smaller in size than people today. The main cause of this was the poor diet and living conditions in 1700s England. The children born in Australia in the early 19th century were much taller and healthier than their parents because of the better diet, sunshine and clean air and water.
